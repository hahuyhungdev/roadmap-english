### 1. How do you prepare data before salary negotiation?

**Common ideas:** market data, role scope, impact, range, alternatives.
**Useful language:** I prepare by collecting..., My number is based on....
**Pattern to apply:** data -> range -> ask.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
Before salary negotiation, I try to collect market range, my recent impact, and the scope of the role. I do not want to speak only from emotion or hope. Good preparation makes the conversation calmer. That helps me a lot.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
My preparation usually starts with three things: market reference, my own evidence, and the context of the role. I want to understand what similar positions are paying, what results I can honestly point to, and what the company is actually asking from the role. This gives me a more realistic range. It also makes me less nervous because I am not speaking from nothing. Preparation turns the conversation into a business discussion, not just a personal wish.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
Before salary negotiation, I prepare data from both outside and inside the company context. Outside data includes market range, role level, and location expectations. Inside data includes my scope, recent impact, delivery quality, and any responsibilities that go beyond the basic role description. The trade-off is that salary conversations are never purely mathematical, but strong preparation still improves clarity and confidence. I want the discussion to be grounded in value and context, not only in feeling.

### 2. What do you say when your salary expectation is above current budget?

**Common ideas:** budget gap, flexible structure, value, alternatives.
**Useful language:** I understand the budget, but based on scope..., Could we discuss...?.
**Pattern to apply:** acknowledge -> restate value -> option.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
If my expectation is above current budget, I try to stay calm and open. I would explain the basis for my expectation and then ask whether there is flexibility in compensation or other parts of the offer. That keeps the conversation constructive. It is better than becoming defensive.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
When my salary expectation is above budget, I try not to turn it into a hard emotional reaction. I would explain why my expectation is at that level and ask whether there is room to adjust salary, bonus, review timing, or role scope. I want to understand the company's real limit before deciding anything. That makes the conversation feel more practical. Even if the answer stays no, the discussion can still stay respectful.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
If my salary expectation is above the current budget, I would respond by clarifying the reasoning behind my number and asking whether there is flexibility elsewhere in the offer structure. That could include salary, bonus, growth path, review timing, or other meaningful components. The trade-off is that I may not get the exact number I want immediately. But staying structured and open gives both sides more room to explore a workable outcome. I think that is more effective than treating the first gap like the end of the conversation.

### 3. How do you respond to critical feedback without becoming defensive?

**Common ideas:** feedback, pause, clarify, avoid defense, next action.
**Useful language:** I try to listen first, Can you give one example?.
**Pattern to apply:** feedback -> clarification -> action.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
I try to listen fully before explaining anything. Then I ask questions to understand the feedback more clearly. That helps me respond to the content instead of reacting to the feeling. It keeps the conversation more useful.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
When I receive critical feedback, I try not to answer too quickly, especially if the comment hits something sensitive. I listen, ask for examples, and make sure I understand the real issue before I defend my side. Usually that lowers the emotional pressure. I think defensiveness grows when the feedback still feels vague or personal. So clarity helps me stay calmer.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
I respond to critical feedback by slowing down and treating the first job as understanding, not self-protection. I listen for the real concern, ask for examples, and try to separate useful feedback from the emotional discomfort of hearing it. The trade-off is that this can feel vulnerable in the moment. But if I react too early, I may protect my ego and lose the value of the feedback. I think strong response starts with clarity before explanation.

### 4. How do you turn feedback into a measurable action plan?

**Common ideas:** action plan, behavior, metric, timeline, follow-up.
**Useful language:** I turn feedback into..., I will measure it by....
**Pattern to apply:** feedback -> action -> metric -> review.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
I turn feedback into action by making it specific and observable. Then I choose one or two behaviors to change and decide how I will track them. That makes improvement easier to see. Without that step, feedback stays too general.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
For me, the key is moving from vague feedback to visible behavior. If someone says my communication needs improvement, I ask what exact part should change. Then I create a small action plan, like clearer status updates, better meeting summaries, or more direct risk signals. I also define how I will know it is improving. That can be feedback from others or fewer repeated misunderstandings.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
I turn feedback into a measurable action plan by translating it into specific behaviors, timeline, and review points. First, I clarify what the feedback actually means in practice. Then I choose a small number of actions that I can repeat consistently and observe over time. The trade-off is that this takes more discipline than simply agreeing with feedback in the moment. But without measurement or follow-up, improvement often becomes only a good intention. I want feedback to change behavior, not just reflection.

### 5. What are the benefits and limits of negotiating every offer?

**Common ideas:** negotiating offers, confidence, relationship risk.
**Useful language:** Negotiating helps when..., The limit is....
**Pattern to apply:** benefit -> limit -> context.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
Negotiating every offer can improve compensation and show confidence. That is a real benefit. But the limit is that not every situation has real flexibility, and not every negotiation adds value. So I think context matters.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
I understand why people say to negotiate every offer, because sometimes even one respectful conversation can improve the outcome. It can also help candidates understand the company's flexibility and priorities. But I do not think it should become a rigid rule. Some offers are already fair, and some situations are more about role fit than pushing every number. I prefer thoughtful negotiation over automatic negotiation.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
The benefit of negotiating every offer is that it encourages candidates to understand their value and not accept the first number passively. It can improve compensation and create a stronger sense of agency. The limit is that negotiation should still be grounded in context, leverage, and quality of fit. The trade-off is confidence versus rigidity. If negotiation becomes automatic and disconnected from reality, it can weaken trust or distract from more important parts of the offer. I think strategic negotiation is stronger than performative negotiation.

### 6. When should growth opportunity matter more than immediate compensation?

**Common ideas:** growth opportunity, compensation, long-term value.
**Useful language:** Growth should matter more when..., But compensation still matters if....
**Pattern to apply:** growth value -> money boundary -> decision.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
Growth opportunity should matter more when the role can clearly improve long-term value and the compensation is still reasonable enough to live with. If the growth is real, it can create bigger returns later. But the present still matters. So balance is important.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
I think growth opportunity matters more when the role gives real learning, stronger scope, or better long-term direction, and when the salary is not creating financial stress. In that case, the slower money today may support stronger progress later. But I do not think people should use growth as an excuse to ignore very weak compensation. It has to be a meaningful opportunity, not just a nice story.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
Growth opportunity should matter more than immediate compensation when the role creates clear long-term leverage and the short-term pay is still acceptable for a stable life. That could mean better mentorship, stronger scope, more meaningful ownership, or a learning curve that changes future options. The trade-off is present security versus future upside. I think it is worth choosing growth when the opportunity is real and the compensation gap is still sustainable. Otherwise, the decision can become idealistic in a way that is hard to maintain.

### 7. How should feedback style differ between junior and senior engineers?

**Common ideas:** junior feedback, senior feedback, specificity, ownership.
**Useful language:** Juniors need more guidance, Seniors need sharper ownership feedback.
**Pattern to apply:** junior style -> senior style -> purpose.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
Feedback for junior and senior engineers should both be honest, but the focus can be different. Junior feedback may need more teaching and structure. Senior feedback may need more depth around judgment and broader impact. The style should match the level of responsibility.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
I think feedback for junior engineers should be clear, specific, and supportive enough to help learning grow. For senior engineers, feedback often needs to be more direct about judgment, influence, and wider team effect. That does not mean one group deserves softness and the other deserves harshness. It just means the feedback should match the kind of decisions they are expected to make. Good feedback feels fair, not generic.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
Feedback style should change with scope of ownership, not only years of experience. Junior engineers often need more concrete examples, more teaching context, and clearer links between behavior and growth. Senior engineers usually need feedback that goes beyond execution into judgment, leadership effect, and broader delivery quality. The trade-off is support versus challenge. I think both levels need honesty, but the shape of that honesty should match the real responsibility they carry.

### 8. You said you are flexible. What is your true non-negotiable point?

**Common ideas:** non-negotiable, flexible areas, real boundary.
**Useful language:** I am flexible on..., but not on....
**Pattern to apply:** flexibility -> boundary -> reason.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
If I say I am flexible, my true non-negotiable point is usually around basic fairness and sustainability. I can be open on structure, but not on conditions that damage trust or stability. That is the line I want to keep clear. Flexibility should still have a boundary.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
My true non-negotiable point is usually not one exact number. It is whether the overall offer still respects the value of the role and supports a sustainable working situation. I can be flexible on details, but not in a way that makes the offer feel misaligned or unbalanced. For me, clarity and respect matter as much as the final amount. That is where flexibility ends.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
If I say I am flexible, my true non-negotiable point is the boundary where the offer stops feeling fair and sustainable relative to the role, scope, and market context. I may be open on structure, timing, or some components of the package. But I do not want flexibility to become quiet self-undervaluation. The trade-off is openness versus self-respect. I think strong negotiation means being cooperative without losing the line that protects real fairness.

### 9. If feedback is vague, how do you avoid fake improvement?

**Common ideas:** vague feedback, examples, behavior, success criteria.
**Useful language:** If feedback is vague, I ask for....
**Pattern to apply:** vague input -> clarifying question -> measurable change.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
If feedback is vague, I try to ask for examples and clearer expectations before acting on it. Otherwise improvement can become performative. I do not want to change randomly just to look responsive. Specificity matters.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
Vague feedback can create fake improvement very easily because I might work hard on the wrong thing. So I usually ask what exact behavior needs to change, what good would look like, and where the gap was visible. This makes the improvement more real. Without that step, I may only become more self-conscious, not actually better. I want clarity before action.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
To avoid fake improvement, I treat vague feedback as incomplete, not as immediately actionable truth. I ask for examples, context, and a clearer picture of the desired behavior before building a plan. The trade-off is that this can make the conversation slightly longer or less comfortable in the moment. But it protects me from changing in a random or performative way just to show that I listened. Real improvement needs enough clarity to be judged honestly.

### 10. If negotiation succeeds but trust declines, was it still a good outcome?

**Common ideas:** negotiation success, trust cost, relationship quality.
**Useful language:** A successful negotiation is not only the number.
**Pattern to apply:** outcome -> trust cost -> judgment.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
Not fully. A successful negotiation should improve the offer without damaging the relationship too much. If trust drops a lot, then part of the outcome is weak. Money matters, but so does the quality of the relationship.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
I would not call that a fully good outcome. If negotiation gets the number I want but leaves the relationship colder or less respectful, then something important was lost. That does not always mean the negotiation was wrong. But it means the process may have been too aggressive or too poorly handled on one side. A strong result should protect both fairness and trust if possible.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
If negotiation succeeds financially but trust declines meaningfully, I would see it as a mixed outcome rather than a clear win. The trade-off is immediate gain versus long-term relationship quality. In some cases, the financial improvement may still be worth it. But if trust drops too much, that can affect future collaboration, growth conversations, and even daily comfort in the role. I think the best negotiation outcome improves the offer while still leaving both sides able to respect the relationship.

### 11. Which part of negotiation is hardest for you in English?

**Common ideas:** English negotiation difficulty, wording, confidence.
**Useful language:** The hardest part is saying..., without sounding....
**Pattern to apply:** language challenge -> phrase -> practice.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
The hardest part for me is sounding firm without sounding too blunt. I want to be clear about my expectation, but still respectful. That balance is not always easy in English. I still practice it.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
For me, the hardest part is staying natural when I need to defend my number. I can explain the logic, but I want it to sound calm and confident, not memorized or too sharp. In English, that takes more control than normal work discussion. I think negotiation language needs both clarity and tone. That is the part I work on most.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
The part I find hardest is expressing firm boundaries in a way that still feels warm and professional. In English, I want to avoid sounding either too passive or too aggressive, especially in compensation discussions. The trade-off is clarity versus tone control. If I focus only on politeness, I may sound uncertain. If I focus only on firmness, I may sound harder than I intend. So this is one area where I still practice phrasing very deliberately.

### 13. In long-term growth, what matters more: pay progression or feedback discipline?

**Common ideas:** pay progression vs feedback discipline, long-term growth.
**Useful language:** Pay progression matters, but feedback discipline compounds.
**Pattern to apply:** compare -> choose -> long-term effect.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
In long-term growth, I think feedback discipline matters a bit more. Pay progression is important, but feedback discipline shapes how much stronger I actually become. That affects future pay too. So I would choose feedback discipline first.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
I still care about pay progression, but I think feedback discipline matters more over time. Better pay can improve life now, but disciplined learning from feedback changes the quality of my future decisions and future value. That effect compounds more deeply. So if I had to choose one as the stronger long-term factor, I would choose feedback discipline. It builds more than one salary jump.

**Version 3 (Career-ready answer):**
For long-term growth, I believe feedback discipline matters more because it strengthens the person who will later create stronger value and better compensation. Pay progression is important and real, but if I cannot absorb and use feedback well, my growth curve may become much flatter over time. The trade-off is short-term gain versus long-term capability building. I think disciplined response to feedback improves judgment, communication, and adaptability, and those things support better outcomes for many years.
