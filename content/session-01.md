# Session 1: Your Role & Work

**Level:** B1+  
**Focus:** Speaking naturally and confidently about your role, responsibilities, collaboration, and impact as a Frontend Developer.

<details open>
<summary><strong>1) Vocabulary </strong></summary>

- **role** (n) – your function or position in a team  
  _Example:_ My role is to build frontend features that are both usable and maintainable.

- **ownership** (n) – taking full responsibility for an area  
  _Example:_ I take ownership of the checkout flow from implementation to monitoring.

- **responsibility** (n) – something you are expected to do  
  _Example:_ One of my core responsibilities is turning design specs into production-ready UI.

- **stakeholder** (n) – someone who has an interest in project results  
  _Example:_ I align expectations with stakeholders before we start a major feature.

- **priority** (n) – the level of importance of a task  
  _Example:_ We review priorities every sprint planning meeting.

- **deadline** (n) – the latest date by which work must be completed  
  _Example:_ I break tasks into smaller milestones to hit deadlines safely.

- **dependency** (n) – something you need before you can continue  
  _Example:_ Frontend work was blocked by an API dependency from another team.

- **handover** (n) – transferring work or context to another person  
  _Example:_ A clear handover note helped the next developer continue quickly.

- **quality** (n) – the standard of work  
  _Example:_ We protect quality with code reviews, tests, and lint rules.

- **impact** (n) – the measurable effect of your work  
  _Example:_ The new dashboard had a positive impact on user retention.

**Additional useful terms:**
- **deliverable** (n) – an output expected at a certain time
- **alignment** (n) – shared understanding across teams
- **trade-off** (n) – balancing two competing options
- **incident** (n) – an unexpected issue in production
- **SLA** (n) – service level agreement for reliability/response expectations

<summary><strong>2) Grammar & Useful Patterns (B1+ to B2 Bridge)</strong></summary>

- **Present Simple for routine work**  
  I usually start my day by checking tickets and sprint priorities.

- **Present Continuous for current sprint tasks**  
  This week, I am working on a new analytics dashboard.

- **Present Perfect for achievements**  
  We have improved page performance by reducing bundle size.

- **Sequencing (first, then, after that, finally)**  
  First, I clarify requirements. Then I split the task. After that, I implement and test it.

- **Cause and result (because, so, as a result)**  
  We improved error handling, so support tickets dropped significantly.

- **Relative clauses**  
  I often work on features that directly affect customer conversion.

### Useful Sentence Patterns
- In my current role, I am responsible for...
- A typical day for me includes...
- I usually coordinate with... to make sure...
- One challenge I often face is..., so I...
- A recent example was when...
- As a result, we were able to...

### Useful Phrasal Verbs

- **work on** → I work on customer-facing features and UI reliability improvements.
- **deal with** → I deal with blockers by syncing early with related teams.
- **follow up on** → I always follow up on post-release issues with support data.
- **hand over** → I hand over clear notes when another developer takes over a task.
- **step in** → I step in to support teammates when deadlines are tight.
- **push back on** → I push back on unclear requirements to avoid rework later.

<summary><strong>4) Typical Dialogues</strong></summary>

### Dialogue 1 – Role Introduction

**Teacher:** Tell me about your job. What do you do?

**You:**  
I work as a frontend developer in a cross-functional product team. My main responsibilities include implementing new UI features, improving performance, and maintaining existing components. I collaborate with designers, backend engineers, QA, and product managers to make sure we deliver features that are useful and stable. I also track release quality after deployment to catch issues early.

### Dialogue 2 – Handling a Changing Priority

**Teacher:** What do you do when a high-priority request appears in the middle of a sprint?

**You:**  
First, I review the impact with the product owner and tech lead. Then we decide whether to swap out a lower-priority item or adjust the scope. I usually break the request into a minimal first version so we can deliver quickly without hurting quality. After release, we follow up with improvements in the next sprint.

### Dialogue 3 – Collaboration Challenge

**Teacher:** Tell me about a time collaboration was difficult.

**You:**  
In one project, design and backend timelines were misaligned, so frontend implementation was delayed. I organized a short alignment meeting, listed dependencies, and proposed a phased rollout. We shipped the core flow first and finished advanced features later. As a result, we met the launch date and avoided last-minute stress.

### Dialogue 4 – Handling Objection

**Teacher:** Isn’t frontend development just about making things look nice?

**You:**  
Frontend is not only about visuals. We handle interaction logic, validation, accessibility, performance, and edge cases. If the frontend is poorly built, users may abandon the product even if backend services are strong. So our work directly affects conversion, retention, and customer trust.

<summary><strong>5) Reading Text</strong></summary>

A frontend developer’s role goes beyond writing UI code. In modern product teams, frontend engineers help translate business goals into practical user flows. They collaborate with multiple stakeholders, manage dependencies, and make trade-offs between speed and quality.

Strong communication is as important as technical skill. Developers who can explain progress clearly, raise risks early, and propose practical alternatives usually deliver more consistently. They also help teams avoid unnecessary rework.

When developers take ownership, the impact becomes measurable: fewer production issues, better user satisfaction, and faster delivery cycles. That is why understanding your role in a broader business context is essential for career growth.

</details>

<summary><strong>6) List of Questions + Ideas</strong></summary>

### Core Questions (must-practice)

1. What is your role, and what are your main responsibilities?
2. What does a typical working day look like for you?
3. Who do you collaborate with most, and how do you work together?
4. How does your work impact users or business outcomes?

### High-Value Case Questions

5. Tell me about a recent feature you delivered. (Situation -> Action -> Result)
6. Describe a time priorities changed mid-sprint. How did you respond?
7. Share a production issue you handled and what you learned.

### Critical Discussion Questions

8. Some people think frontend is easier than backend. What is your view?
9. How do you balance delivery speed and quality under tight deadlines?
10. What makes a frontend developer valuable beyond coding skills?

**Tips for speaking practice:**
- Use at least one real project example in each answer.
- Keep your structure clear: Situation -> Action -> Result.
- Mention one measurable outcome whenever possible.
