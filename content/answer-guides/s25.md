### 5. What are the benefits and limits of strict STAR structure in interviews?

**Version 1 (Base):**  
Strict STAR structure helps keep answers clear and easy to follow. That is a big benefit. But the limit is that it can sound too mechanical if I follow it too rigidly. So I use it as support, not as a script.

**Version 2 (Natural):**  
I think STAR is useful because it stops interview answers from becoming messy or too abstract. It gives me a clear path through the story. But if I follow it too strictly, I can sound memorized and lose natural flow. So I like using STAR as a frame, not as a cage. The structure helps, but the answer still needs real voice.

**Version 3 (Interview):**  
The biggest benefit of strict STAR structure is clarity. It helps candidates stay grounded in a real example and makes it easier for the interviewer to follow the situation, action, and result. The limit is that overly strict STAR can sound robotic and reduce authentic conversation. The trade-off is structure versus natural rhythm. I think STAR is strongest when it supports a real answer, not when it becomes more visible than the story itself.

### 6. When should interview answers prioritize technical depth vs communication clarity?

**Version 1 (Base):**  
Interview answers should prioritize communication clarity first, then add technical depth where it matters. If the structure is unclear, the depth may not land well. I want the listener to follow the logic before the details. That order works better for me.

**Version 2 (Natural):**  
I think interview answers should start with clarity and then adjust the depth based on the role and the question. If the interviewer cannot follow the main logic, technical detail loses value. But if the role needs stronger engineering judgment, then depth still matters a lot. So I try to make the answer easy to follow first, and then go deeper where it adds real value. That feels more balanced.

**Version 3 (Interview):**  
Interview answers should prioritize communication clarity first because even strong technical ideas can fail if the structure is weak or confusing. After that, the right amount of technical depth depends on the role, interviewer, and question type. The trade-off is simplicity versus precision. If I stay too simple, the answer may feel shallow. If I go too deep too early, I may lose the audience. So I try to build a clear answer first and then layer depth on top of it where it strengthens the story.

### 7. How should mock interview strategy differ for beginners vs experienced engineers?

**Version 1 (Base):**  
Beginners often need more structure and repetition around core question types. Experienced engineers may need more refinement around depth, influence, and follow-up handling. The strategy should match the gap. Not everyone needs the same kind of practice.

**Version 2 (Natural):**  
For beginners, mock interviews should focus more on building basic structure, confidence, and clear examples. The main goal is often reducing confusion and helping answers feel more stable. Experienced engineers usually need a different kind of practice. They may already have enough structure, but still need sharper trade-offs, stronger depth, or better handling of follow-up pressure. So the strategy should change with the level.

**Version 3 (Interview):**  
Mock interview strategy should differ because the core weakness is usually different at each stage. Beginners often need more help with structure, confidence, and turning experience into simple usable stories. Experienced engineers often need more pressure-testing around judgment, influence, follow-up questions, and sharper role fit. The trade-off is foundation versus refinement. I think good practice should target the real gap, not treat every candidate as if they need the same kind of repetition.

I would rather have slightly less perfect fluency with stronger proof than the other way around.
