**Version 1 (Base):**  
Heavy mock practice can improve structure, fluency, and confidence. That is the clear benefit. But the limit is that too much repetition can make answers sound mechanical. So the practice still needs variation and reflection.

**Version 2 (Natural):**  
I think heavy mock practice is useful because it reduces hesitation and helps weak patterns become visible faster. It also gives more chances to test recovery after mistakes. But the limit is that too much repetition can make answers too polished and less real. If I only optimize for mock comfort, I may lose freshness and honest thinking. So volume helps, but quality of practice still matters a lot.

**Version 3 (Interview):**  
The benefit of heavy mock-interview practice is that it builds familiarity, answer control, and resilience under pressure. It also makes weak points much easier to identify because patterns repeat quickly. The limit is over-rehearsal. The trade-off is confidence versus authenticity. If I practice too heavily without variation, my answers may become smooth but less alive, and that can hurt real interviews. I think strong mock practice needs both repetition and enough freshness to keep the thinking real.

### 6. When should candidates prioritize fluency, and when should they prioritize content depth?

**Version 1 (Base):**  
Candidates should prioritize fluency when basic delivery is still blocking the message. They should prioritize content depth when the answer sounds smooth but still feels weak or shallow. Both matter, but the order depends on the real gap. That is how I see it.

**Version 2 (Natural):**  
I think fluency should come first when a candidate still struggles to complete answers clearly or naturally. Without enough fluency, strong content may never land well. But once the delivery is stable enough, content depth becomes more important because interviewers need real evidence and judgment. So I would prioritize the bottleneck, not the ideal final version. The gap decides the focus.

**Version 3 (Interview):**  
Candidates should prioritize fluency when their current delivery is too broken to carry the message consistently. They should prioritize content depth when they already sound understandable but still lack strong examples, real ownership, or trade-off thinking. The trade-off is surface delivery versus durable substance. I think the right priority depends on what currently limits the interview most. For me, fluency helps the answer arrive, but depth is what makes it credible.
